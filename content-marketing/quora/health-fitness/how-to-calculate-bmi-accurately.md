# Quora Answer: How Do I Calculate My BMI Accurately? What's a Healthy Weight?

**Quora Question:** "I'm trying to lose weight and want to know if I'm in a healthy range. How accurate is BMI? Should I use it?"

**Word Count:** 1200 words

---

## Answer:

Good question. BMI is widely used but often misunderstood. Let me break down exactly how it works, why it matters, and how to use it correctly.

### What is BMI?

BMI = Body Mass Index. It's a simple calculation:

**Formula:**
```
BMI = Weight (kg) / Height (m)²

Or in imperial:
BMI = Weight (lbs) × 703 / Height (inches)²
```

**Example:**
- Height: 5'10" (70 inches)
- Weight: 200 lbs

BMI = (200 × 703) / (70²) = 140,600 / 4,900 = **28.7**

### BMI Categories (What Your Number Means)

| BMI Range | Category |
|---|---|
| Under 18.5 | Underweight |
| 18.5 - 24.9 | Normal/Healthy |
| 25.0 - 29.9 | Overweight |
| 30.0+ | Obese |

**Your Example (BMI 28.7):**
You're in "Overweight" category. Healthy range for your height: 132-178 lbs.

### Why BMI Matters (And Why It Doesn't, Always)

**Why It's Useful:**

1. **Population-Level Health Tracking**
   - Governments use it to track obesity trends
   - Doctors use it as a screening tool
   - Useful for comparing large groups

2. **Baseline Health Assessment**
   - Quick check for whether you're in healthy range
   - Simple number to track over time
   - Shows progress toward weight goals

3. **Insurance & Medical Purposes**
   - Many insurers use BMI for risk assessment
   - Doctors use it to identify health risks
   - Not perfect, but standard reference

4. **Weight Goal Setting**
   - Gives you target range to work toward
   - Helps avoid unrealistic expectations
   - Motivates when you see progress

**Why It Has Limitations:**

1. **Doesn't Account for Muscle**
   - Muscular athletes can have "overweight" BMI
   - Muscle weighs more than fat
   - BMI doesn't distinguish between them

   Example: Professional bodybuilder
   - Height: 5'10"
   - Weight: 220 lbs (mostly muscle)
   - BMI: 31.5 (officially "obese")
   - Health: Excellent
   - Issue: BMI misclassifies him

2. **Doesn't Account for Age**
   - Muscle naturally decreases with age
   - Body composition changes
   - Same BMI means different health at age 25 vs 65

3. **Doesn't Account for Distribution**
   - Fat around waist = health risk
   - Fat around hips = different risk
   - BMI treats all weight equally

4. **Doesn't Account for Bone Density**
   - Some people have naturally denser bones
   - Can skew BMI without affecting health

### How to Calculate Your BMI (Accurately)

**Using an Online Calculator (Easiest):**

1. Go to BMI calculator
2. Enter height (feet/inches or cm)
3. Enter weight (lbs or kg)
4. Click "Calculate"
5. See your BMI and category

Takes 10 seconds. No math required.

**Manual Calculation (If You Prefer):**

Imperial units:
```
BMI = (Weight in lbs × 703) / (Height in inches)²

Example: 5'10", 200 lbs
= (200 × 703) / (70²)
= 140,600 / 4,900
= 28.7
```

Metric units:
```
BMI = Weight (kg) / Height (m)²

Example: 178cm, 91kg
= 91 / (1.78²)
= 91 / 3.17
= 28.7
```

### What's Your Healthy Weight Range?

**Formula:**
Healthy BMI range: 18.5 - 24.9

```
Lower healthy weight:
Height² × 18.5 = Lower limit

Upper healthy weight:
Height² × 24.9 = Upper limit
```

**Example (5'10" / 70 inches):**
- Lower: 70² × 18.5 / 703 = 132 lbs
- Upper: 70² × 24.9 / 703 = 178 lbs
- Healthy range: 132-178 lbs

Most BMI calculators show this automatically.

### Better Assessment Tools (Beyond BMI)

If you want a more complete picture:

1. **Waist Circumference**
   - Measure waist at narrowest point
   - Health risk: >40 inches (men), >35 inches (women)
   - Indicates visceral fat (dangerous type)

2. **Body Fat Percentage**
   - More accurate than BMI
   - Requires measurement (scale, calipers, dexa scan)
   - Healthy: 15-25% (men), 25-32% (women)

3. **Fitness Level**
   - Can you run 1 mile?
   - Can you do 10 pushups?
   - Fitness matters more than exact weight

4. **Overall Health Markers**
   - Blood pressure
   - Cholesterol
   - Blood sugar
   - More important than weight alone

### Using BMI for Weight Loss (Practical Approach)

**Step 1: Calculate Your Current BMI**
Use online calculator. Takes 10 seconds.

**Step 2: Know Your Healthy Range**
For your height, what BMI range is "normal"? Most calculators show this.

**Step 3: Set Realistic Goal**
Don't aim for 120 lbs if you're naturally built for 160. Aim for within healthy range.

**Step 4: Track Progress**
Weigh monthly (not weekly). Track BMI trend:
- Started: BMI 30.2
- Month 2: BMI 29.8
- Month 4: BMI 28.5
- Trending right direction

**Step 5: Adjust Expectations**
Healthy loss: 1-2 lbs per week. BMI drops 0.1-0.2 per week. That's progress, celebrate it.

### Red Flags (When BMI Isn't Telling Full Story)

1. **You're very muscular**
   → Use body fat % instead

2. **You're elderly**
   → Slightly higher BMI may be normal, ask doctor

3. **You have significant disability**
   → BMI may not apply, talk to doctor

4. **You're pregnant**
   → Different standards apply

5. **Your health markers are all great**
   → Follow doctor's assessment over BMI

### FAQ

**Q: Is BMI accurate for children?**
A: No. Children use age-adjusted charts (different percentiles by age).

**Q: Should I aim for the middle of healthy range?**
A: No hard rule. Natural weight varies. Aim for where you feel good and have energy.

**Q: If my BMI is normal but I feel unhealthy?**
A: See a doctor. BMI is one metric, not the only one.

**Q: How often should I recalculate?**
A: Monthly if actively trying to lose weight. Quarterly if maintaining.

**Q: Does BMI account for fitness?**
A: No. Very fit people can have "high" BMI due to muscle.

## My Recommendation

BMI is useful as a **starting point**, not the final answer:

1. **Calculate your BMI** (online, 10 seconds)
2. **Note your healthy range** for your height
3. **Use it to track progress** over months
4. **Supplement with other metrics** (how you feel, energy level, fitness)
5. **Talk to doctor** if significantly overweight/underweight

For your weight loss journey, BMI is a helpful tool. Use it to set realistic goals and track progress. But remember: it's one of several ways to assess health.
